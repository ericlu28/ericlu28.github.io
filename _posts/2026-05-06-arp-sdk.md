## Context

### Fraud today

Fraud is a [$138.5 billion problem](https://capitaloneshopping.com/research/ecommerce-fraud-statistics/)- and it's accelerating. U.S. companies lost [~10% of revenue to fraud in 2025, up 46% from the year before](https://newsroom.transunion.com/h2-2025-global-fraud-report/). We've seen a [1,210% surge in AI-driven fraud](https://www.vectra.ai/topics/ai-scams) over the same period.

The incidents are getting harder to ignore:
- Robinhood [was hit with large-scale phishing attacks](https://www.thestreet.com/crypto/markets/robinhood-suffers-phishing-attempt-ahead-of-quarterly-earnings) targeting its user base
- Meta took action against [1 billion fake accounts in Q1 2025 alone](https://transparency.meta.com/reports/community-standards-enforcement/fake-accounts/facebook)
- Stripe's [2025 State of AI and Fraud report](https://stripe.com/lp/2025-state-of-ai-and-fraud) found free trial abuse grew 6.2x in just four months- AI startups are seeing 10x more abuse than enterprise companies

Fraudsters are crawling the web at historic rates. Malicious bots now make up [~37% of all internet traffic](https://www.wired.com/story/ai-bots-are-now-a-significant-source-of-web-traffic/), used for things like:
- **API reconnaissance**: mapping hidden access points to services
- **Credential stuffing**: testing stolen passwords, SSNs, and credit card numbers at scale
- **Vulnerability probing**: automated scanning for exploitable surface area
- **DDoS**: overwhelming services to degrade or deny availability

Honestly, you don't even need me to tell you that fraud is an issue. We've all heard the personal stories from our friends about some deep-fake phone call to their grandpa or some well-written email.

### Adobe Risk Platform

At Adobe, we've similarly seen a 10x increase in fraud attempts between last year and now.

I work on the Adobe Risk Platform. We sit between end users and Adobe's core products, providing fraud detection and risk assessment at scale. Without sharing too much, it's clear to us that attackers' tools have gotten sharper, and the tools we use to detect and respond also need to keep pace.

We're in a never-ending battle of whack-a-mole, balancing plugging the holes mitigation with building solutions that prevent future problems we haven't even seen- left-shifting the fraud prevention.

### Fraud detection SDK

That's where the fraud detection SDK comes in. It's our primary instrumentation layer in the browser- collecting signals, enforcing policy, and orchestrating third-party fraud detection vendors. If it's running stale code, we're flying blind.

And that was exactly the problem. Even though we frequently released updated versions of the SDK with new features, bug fixes, and configuration changes, clients were routinely served stale versions. There was no way to force them onto the latest release. For a team whose job is to respond to a constantly shifting threat landscape, that's a meaningful gap.

I had just joined the team in Q4 2025. The SDK was a few years old. This felt like a good opportunity to modernize it- and prove my worth in the process.

## Problem Space

The old integration model was simple: a single minified script behind a stable URL... easy to embed, but it collides with how caching works. Browsers and CDNs cache stable URLs aggressively, so after shipping a new feature or patching a detection rule, not every session is guaranteed to run the new code right away. For a risk platform SDK, version skew is a correctness problem: you can't trust a source of truth that is fixed for only some users.

The embedded SDK also sits in environments not under our control. Assuming the worst, single-page applications can load our SDK multiple times, and third-party scripts loading asynchronously can fail in ways that surface as our bugs.

Those symptoms also blur together in operations. An incident can be a vendor outage, a misconfiguration, or a stale cached script- unless "what ran" and "what policy applied" are easy to separate, you're debugging all three at once.

Short-term mitigations- shorter cache TTLs, "call init only once" in the docs, cache-busting query params... these things help at the margins. They don't establish the invariant we needed: a stable entrypoint embedders can keep forever, and a versioned implementation that caches safely and rolls out predictably.

A separate but related problem: without a singleton guard, SPA environments can contain multiple components with competing SDK initializations. Feature flags, vendor orchestration, and policy evaluation all need to flow from one coherent initialization. Fingerprint Pro and Stripe.js handle this with implicit singletons- basically "store the promise and reuse it." We needed the same guarantee, but explicit and enforced by the SDK itself, not developer discipline.

Beyond delivery and initialization, there were a handful of other problems worth fixing in the same revamp: blocking the main thread during vendor loading, high-frequency log flushing, and vendor integrations that had grown into a tangle of nested callbacks with no clear lifecycle.

## Delivery

The core idea: split what integrators embed from what actually runs.

The old monolithic script became two artifacts: a lightweight loader (~1-2KB) and a content-hashed implementation bundle. When the implementation changes, the hash- and thus the URL- changes, but the loader URL never does. Embedders keep the same script tag forever. Versioning happens entirely on our side.

```
Browser requests loader (short CDN TTL, no browser cache)
  → loader injects <script> tag for hashed SDK (long TTL / immutable)
  → SRI check passes
  → SDK runs
```

Caching follows this split deliberately. The loader uses `s-maxage=300, max-age=0, must-revalidate`- CDN-cached for 5 minutes, never browser-cached. Preventing browser caching on the loader is a conscious tradeoff: it costs a fast CDN round-trip (~5-20ms) on every page load, but it means a CDN cache purge is immediately effective for all users. If we push a critical security patch and issue a purge, the next request from any browser picks up the new version. With browser caching in the mix, that guarantee disappears- we'd have no way to reach users who already have the old loader cached locally.

The hashed SDK bundle uses `Cache-Control: public, max-age=31536000, immutable`- safe to cache forever in both CDN and browser, because a new hash means a new URL, which is an automatic cache bust.

**Integrity at load time**: the build process computes an SHA-256 digest of the hashed SDK bundle and bakes it into the loader as a [Subresource Integrity](https://developer.mozilla.org/en-US/docs/Web/Security/Subresource_Integrity) attribute. If the CDN serves a tampered file, the browser rejects it natively before executing a single byte. For a fraud detection SDK, this is non-negotiable.

**Resilience**: if loading the hashed script fails from a transient network glitch, the loader retries once before surfacing the failure. The SRI and retry are coupled: if SRI verification fails, `onerror` fires just as it would for a network failure, and the retry re-fetches from the CDN. If a CDN edge node is consistently serving a bad file, both attempts fail and the SDK does not load- which is the correct behavior. Running no fraud detection is safer than running tampered fraud detection.

**Origin resilience**: the loader also carries `stale-if-error=86400`. If the origin server is unreachable during CDN revalidation, the CDN continues serving the last known good loader for up to 24 hours. A stale loader still points to a valid previously-deployed SDK hash, which is still cached immutably at the CDN edge. Fraud protection stays active during an origin outage.

**Rollback**: because we retain the last N hashed bundles on the server, rollback is a loader redeployment- point the loader at a previous hash. The old bundle is still on the CDN with an immutable TTL. Within 5 minutes (the loader's CDN TTL), clients pick up the previous version.

---

Delivery solves which file runs. The handoff solves when host code can call into the SDK.

The loader installs a stub on `window` before the hashed bundle has finished downloading. That stub accepts calls like `initAsync` and queues them against a promise that resolves when the real SDK loads. As an optimization, the stub also immediately fires a `fetch()` for the client's vendor configuration from CDN when `initAsync` is called- before the SDK bundle has even arrived. That prefetch runs in parallel with the SDK download, so by the time the real SDK loads and processes the config, the response is either already in-flight or already cached.

Early callers don't need custom `onload` choreography or race-prone "poll until the global exists" loops. They invoke the same API they'd use post-load, and the work is held until the implementation is ready.

When the hashed script runs, it replaces the stub and flushes the queue. From the host's perspective: one stable entrypoint, one stable call pattern, regardless of when the heavy code actually arrives.

This design pairs directly with the singleton enforcement. `initAsync()` flows through a `create()` factory with a private constructor. At most one instance is ever constructed:

```typescript
class SDK {
  private static _instance: SDK | null = null;
  private static _createPromise: Promise<SDK> | null = null;

  static async create(config: SDKConfig): Promise<SDK> {
    if (SDK._instance !== null) {
      SDK._instance._logger.warn('SDK.create() called more than once. Returning existing instance.');
      return SDK._instance;
    }
    if (SDK._createPromise !== null) {
      return SDK._createPromise; // deduplicate concurrent calls
    }

    SDK._createPromise = (async () => {
      const instance = new SDK(config); // sync: logger, config, validation
      const [, vendorConfigResponse] = await Promise.all([
        instance.fetchFeatureFlags(),
        instance.fetchVendorConfig(config._vendorConfigPrefetch),
      ]);
      instance._vendorConfig = instance.applyConfig(vendorConfigResponse);
      instance.loadVendors(); // fire-and-forget
      SDK._instance = instance;
      return instance;
    })();

    return SDK._createPromise;
  }

  private constructor(config: SDKConfig) { /* ... */ }
}
```

The `_createPromise` guard handles the case where two callers race through the `_instance === null` check before either finishes. Both get the same in-flight promise. The singleton reference is only set on success, so a failed `create()` never leaves a partially-constructed instance behind that subsequent callers would silently reuse.

Repeated calls just return the existing instance- no duplicate vendor loads, no duplicate listeners, no race conditions when SPAs call initialization from multiple components.

## Runtime architecture

Initialization runs in three deliberate phases:

**1. Synchronous setup and validation**

The constructor builds the logger and client connectors, then validates config. A bad config- missing `clientId`, a non-function callback, an invalid environment- throws immediately, before any network calls go out. This is intentional: you want synchronous failures to surface right away, not buried inside an async chain. The logger is available from this point forward for every subsequent operation.

**2. Parallel async work**

Feature flags and vendor configuration are fetched together via `Promise.all()`. Feature flags come from our flag service via a BFF (more on this below). Vendor config- a per-client JSON file that determines which third-party integrations to load and how to parameterize them- is fetched from CDN, reusing the prefetch the loader already started.

Neither fetch has a dependency on the other, so there's no reason to run them sequentially. Feature flags must be settled before filtering the vendor config (you need to know which features are enabled before deciding which vendors to activate), but that ordering is enforced by the `Promise.all()`- both resolve before `applyConfig()` runs.

Both fetches degrade safely. If the feature flag fetch fails, the SDK falls back to an empty feature set. If the vendor config fetch fails or returns malformed JSON, the SDK falls back to a hardcoded client config map. Either way, init still succeeds- it's better to initialize with defaults than to fail entirely.

**3. Background vendor loading**

Vendor scripts are loaded after config resolves, but the host gets back a live SDK instance without waiting for any of it. The intentional split: be ready with policy and config first, then orchestrate the heavy third-party work without blocking the caller.

Results arrive asynchronously via `tokenCallback`, `successCallback`, and `errorCallback`. Tokens are composite- as each vendor script loads and returns its signal, the SDK rebuilds the aggregate token and re-invokes `tokenCallback`. The client receives multiple callbacks as vendor tokens trickle in, each one more complete than the last.

## Engineering choices and other things worth naming

**BFF for feature flags, not a client secret in the browser**

Feature flag evaluation needs auth. The naive path is to include a client key in the SDK bundle and let the browser call the flag service directly. That's a non-starter for a risk platform: client secrets embedded in browser code are not secrets. Anyone can open DevTools.

Instead, the browser talks to a backend-for-frontend layer we own, and service-to-service auth is handled server-side. The SDK never holds a credential. This is a standard pattern, but it's worth naming explicitly because the pressure to "just put the key in the config" comes up often when teams want to move fast.

**Vendor calls behind a state machine**

The SDK orchestrates multiple third-party vendors. Without structure, vendor initialization quickly becomes a tangle of nested callbacks and ad-hoc readiness flags- and in a codebase that had grown over several years, that's exactly where we found ourselves.

Each vendor is now modeled as a class with an explicit state machine:

```
Idle → Loading → Ready → TokenReceived
              ↘ Error → (single retry)
```

Transitions are deterministic, and the only way to trigger side effects- calling a vendor API, emitting a token- is through a valid state transition. Vendor instances own their own state, cookies, and performance marks. The SDK holds a `Map<string, IVendor>` typed to an interface, not the concrete class, so test doubles are clean and vendor internals are fully decoupled.

This paid off during incidents: instead of reasoning about which callbacks had fired and in what order, we could read the state enum and know exactly where each vendor stood.

**Long tasks and the main thread**

Third-party vendor scripts have a habit of blocking the main thread in ways that are hard to attribute. We deferred all vendor loading to a background phase, after the SDK instance has already been returned to the caller. If the DOM is still loading when that phase starts, vendor loading also waits for `DOMContentLoaded` before injecting any script tags. The host application stays interactive throughout. [Web.dev's long tasks guide](https://web.dev/articles/optimize-long-tasks) is the clearest writeup on why this matters and how to measure it.

**Log batching**

Sending a network request per log event doesn't scale, and in a high-traffic SDK it adds up fast. Logs are buffered in memory and flushed either on a timer or when the buffer hits a size threshold, whichever comes first. The tradeoff: a crash before a flush can drop logs- acceptable for a telemetry pipeline, not acceptable for audit-critical events, which bypass the buffer and send immediately.

## Lessons learned and closing

**The invariant matters more than the optimization**

A lot of the problems this revamp solved weren't performance problems. They were correctness problems dressed up as performance problems. Stale cached scripts aren't slow- they're wrong. Duplicate SDK initialization isn't inefficient- it's undefined behavior in the host's environment. Framing the work as "establish the invariant first, then optimize" kept the scope from drifting toward premature tuning.

**Stable surface, versioned internals**

The loader/hashed-implementation split is the most transferable pattern from this project. Give embedders a URL they never have to change, and handle versioning entirely on your side. It removes an entire class of coordination problems: embedders don't need to track your releases, and you don't need to negotiate cache TTLs with a dozen different teams. Emergency response- a CDN purge after a critical patch- is effective because browser caching isn't in the way.

If there's one thing I'd carry into any future SDK design: define the integration contract as narrowly as possible, and keep it stable. Everything behind it is yours to evolve.

**Singleton enforcement belongs in the SDK, not the docs**

Most leading SDKs handle the duplicate-initialization problem by documenting it away- "store the promise and reuse it." That relies on developer discipline, which is not a reliable invariant. Encoding the singleton in the SDK itself (private constructor, static factory, in-flight deduplication) makes the correct behavior the only possible behavior, regardless of what the caller does.

**Joining a system mid-life**

I joined the team in Q4 2025 and this was the first major project I owned. Working inside an established system, with existing integrators and production traffic, is different from greenfield work. You don't get to make assumptions. You have to understand why the old design existed before you change it, because the constraints that shaped it are often still real. Some of the patterns I initially read as technical debt turned out to have good reasons behind them- and a few that didn't gave me the justification I needed to rebuild them the right way.
